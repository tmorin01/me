About Me

I recently graduated from Tufts University with a B.S. in Cognitive & Brain Science and Computer Science. In September, I began a PhD program in Computational Neuroscience at Boston University. As an undergraduate, I spent two years working with PET and MRI neuroimaging technologies at the Martinos Center for Biomedical Imaging at Mass. General Hospital. I am interested in machine learning and functional neuroimaging, and hope that my research will contribute to an improved understanding of memory, attention, and other aspects of basic cognition. I am originally from Braintree, MA and have lived in the Boston area my entire life. Outside of lab, I enjoy running and learning American Sign Language. Below, I've included some highlights from past projects that I've worked on. If you have any questions about my research, feel free to get in touch.

Gamma Bomb

Gamma Bomb is a collection of Matlab scripts used to analyze blood data from PET scans. With a simple GUI, users can input custom data and fit several mathematical models to their time activity curves. Additionally, Gamma Bomb performs metabolite correction, background correction, and outputs a useful Quality Control report that allows researchers to check each intermediate step in the analysis.

PK Simulation

PK Simulation Tool is a fully customizable, Matlab-based GUI that allows researchers to run simulations without ever writing a line of code. With the tool, researchers can simulate custom scan-procedures by adjusting the individual kinetic properties of a radiotracer, creating novel infusion paradigms, planning pharmacological challenges, and analyzing the data through several mathematical models. With the PK Simulation Tool, researchers can quickly simulate PET studies, evaluate their feasibility, and optimize experimental designs.

Can a Computer Detect Schizophrenia?

With 95 subjects from an Open fMRI Dataset (accession #ds000115), I created a set of competing Hidden Markov Models designed to calculate the probability that each subject was either Schizophrenic, the Healthy Sibling of a Schizophrenic, or a Healthy Control. The algorithm achieved modest success, correctly classifying 52% of subjects. When Healthy Siblings were removed from the dataset, the success rate increased to 79%.

Economic Diversity at Tufts

Enigma is an independent publication platform based out of Tufts University focused on the crossroads of Computer Science/Data Science and the rest of the world. We surveyed over 450 undergraduates at Tufts University to learn more about the socioeconomic situations students are in and to find out how they felt about economic diversity. Our final report was presented at Enigma's biannual symposium and published online .
